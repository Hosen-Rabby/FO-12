Volunteerverse

01-Overview

This app concept was my submission for the Adobe Creative Jam x Amazon. The goal of this was to create an inclusive interface for highschool students looking for volunteering opportunities. This app is a fully fledged hub that has all the resources highschoolers need to make the most of volunteering.

Duration

2 weeks

October 20 - November 4

Rules

I worked as the UX designer on this project, guiding the testing and information architecture while my teammate was the UI designer responsible for the branding and colours.

The Challange

Inspired by Amazon’s initiative, your challenge is to design an inclusive experience in a third-party mobile app for high school students looking for opportunities to fulfill community service hours or volunteer to help their local community, no matter what their background or ability.

02-Brainstorm

Research

  • We first began this project by brainstorming potential ideas that would appeal to a younger demographic of students grade 8-12.
  • We then listed all the necessary features that an app of our scale should include without overcomplicating things. This would be beneficial later on as we would have many problems with the information architecture.
  • We took a look at currently existing apps on the app store that tracked volunteer hours for a bit of competitive analysis.
  • Finally, we referenced other social media sites that are more common to this younger demographic such as snapchat and instagram.

What we learned

  • Our target demographic for this app would be younger than those of other apps that we were accustomed to creating.
  • As a result, we made the decisive conclusion that our app’s interface would be totally unique compared to other apps that had similar goals.
  • Other related apps were incredibly bland and we immediately realized that this industry had so much potential. So a totally unique idea would not be perceived as something out of the blue.
  • We ascertained a pattern within social media applications and ultimately determined the cause behind their domination of student’s screen time. This was the visually focused design with intuitive controls on a singular, central interface.

03-Concept UX

Preliminary concepts

  • We tried to balance the amount of functions we were trying to add with some simplicity so that the overall cognitive load on each screen would be minimized.
  • We found that a booking functionality and a chatting functionality were the most essential and we then decided to start there.
  • We made the landing page of the site a map based page with our mascot as a welcoming penguin. This visually engaging feature differentiated us from the other competitors.

Other important functions

  • A preference section where users can filter the volunteering opportunities that are visible from the home screen. For example, they can filter out events without wheelchair ramps.
  • A camera feature where they can scan the volunteer proof and save it on the app for quick access.
  • An inclusive avatar section where students of any gender, race, and age can find an extensive variety of customizability to express themselves.

This opens up the usage of our app to a myriad of users that have experienced issues in other apps due to the lack of accessibility options

04 - Test & Refine

Identifying pain points from user research testing

After outlining our requirements for the low-fidelity wireframes, we then created the high-fidelity prototypes in order to perform user testing. The purpose of this testing before finalizing the app was to find any gaps in my reasoning and challenges with the app design. I asked a mentor and a few highschool students to test the prototype of this app, instructing them to go through the main user flow and voice out their concerns.

Reducing complexities created by an overabundance of functionality

We received feedback that the home page had too many icons and overwhelmed many new users regarding the workflow. We eventually grouped the chat button and the friends button in order to reduce the cognitive overload that users were currently experiencing

Inconsistency from screen to screen

Users felt that most sections were too different from one to the next. They were struggling to mentally remember where certain things were located and what purpose they served overall. We eventually found a way to make each screen and section more consistent in terms of text treatment, button styling, and grid pattern.

Hidden affordance with horizontal scrolling within the avatar section

One of our principal functionalities with the avatar section received negative feedback as it was evident that users did not know that they could scroll horizontally which negatively affected their experience. We solved this by making the last item slightly visible so that this would pique the user’s experience, enticing them to scroll and discover what the hidden item was.

05 - Reflection

Information Architecture is important as early as possible

We quickly found that we were having issues with the user flow later in the prototyping process which could have been easily avoided by focusing more on the information architecture as early as possible. Many of our issues were simply compounded over time as we had done a minimum amount of the information architecture necessary until the prototyping stage.

Finding the fine line between functionality and simplicity

Many of our users gave feedback regarding the complexity of our site. We eventually found the optimal balance point by considering the use of tooltips, facilitated by our penguin mascot. We also grouped unnecessary content in an endeavour to simplify the home page for our app.
